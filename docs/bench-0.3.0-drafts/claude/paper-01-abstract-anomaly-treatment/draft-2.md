Detecting anomalous graphs without labels remains difficult: standard one-class and reconstruction-based objectives collapse onto representative substructures and miss the irregular graphs that matter most in molecular screening and financial fraud detection. We propose DVGC, a dual-view graph contrastive pretraining framework that aligns a node-attribute view with a structural-motif view of the same graph; at test time, DVGC produces an anomaly score from the disagreement between the two views under the pretrained encoder, requiring no label access and no per-dataset retraining. Across ten TUDataset benchmarks and two OGB molecular property datasets, DVGC raises mean ROC-AUC by 4.8 points over GOOD-D, the strongest prior self-supervised baseline, and by 9.1 points over one-class graph kernel methods. The gains persist under severe pretraining-data scarcity: when restricted to 20 percent of the normal graphs, DVGC retains 93 percent of its full-data AUC, suggesting the dual-view signal captures structure beyond what density-based methods recover. A controlled study on synthetic graphs with injected edge-rewiring and subgraph-substitution anomalies shows that the two views specialize: the attribute view dominates on feature-level anomalies, while the structural view dominates on topological anomalies, and their disagreement recovers both regimes at a single threshold. Pretrained encoders for all twelve datasets and an open-source implementation compatible with PyTorch Geometric accompany the paper.
