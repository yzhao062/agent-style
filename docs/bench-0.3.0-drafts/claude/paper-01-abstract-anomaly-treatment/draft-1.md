Graph-level anomaly detection identifies molecular, protein, and transaction graphs whose topology or attributes deviate from a reference population, yet label scarcity makes supervised training impractical for most real deployments. Recent self-supervised approaches either inherit node-level objectives that ignore global structure or rely on reconstruction losses that assign low anomaly scores to structurally simple normals. We propose GraphCon-AD, which pretrains a graph encoder with a dual-view contrastive objective that aligns augmented topological and attribute views at the graph level, then scores test graphs by embedding distance to a compact hypersphere fit on pretraining representations. The topological view uses subgraph diffusion while the attribute view perturbs node features under a degree-aware noise schedule, producing views that are semantically consistent but structurally decorrelated, which prevents the encoder from collapsing onto trivial invariances. Across twelve benchmarks, GraphCon-AD improves mean AUC by 7.3 points over the strongest prior self-supervised baseline on the TU-Social suite (ENZYMES, PROTEINS, DD, REDDIT-B), matches or exceeds supervised one-class methods on AIDS and BZR despite using no labels, and reduces false-positive rate at 95% recall by 41% on the Ethereum-Tx fraud-detection benchmark, where anomalies are rare and structurally subtle. Ablations show that removing either view erases roughly half of the gain, and that the degree-aware noise schedule is necessary for robustness on heavy-tailed graphs.
