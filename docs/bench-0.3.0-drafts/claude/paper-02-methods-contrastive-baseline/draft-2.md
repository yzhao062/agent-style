## Methods

Our pipeline comprises three components: a graph encoder that maps each input graph to a fixed-dimensional embedding, a contrastive self-supervised pretraining stage that shapes the embedding space, and an anomaly score computed at inference from the pretrained representations.

The encoder is a five-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN), chosen for its expressive power on graph-level tasks. Each GIN layer applies a two-layer MLP with ReLU activations and batch normalization over aggregated neighbor features, with hidden dimension 128 throughout. Node embeddings from all layers are concatenated and reduced to a graph-level representation via a sum readout, following prior work on graph classification. A two-layer projection head then maps the readout into a 64-dimensional contrastive space, which is used only during pretraining; downstream scoring operates on the raw readout. Parameters are optimized with Adam at a learning rate of $1\mathrm{e}{-3}$ and weight decay of $1\mathrm{e}{-5}$.

For contrastive pretraining we adopt a graph-level InfoNCE objective. Two stochastic augmentations are applied to each input graph to produce a positive pair: edge dropping with probability 0.2 and node feature masking with probability 0.15, sampled independently for each view. Within a mini-batch of 256 graphs, the two views of the same graph form the positive pair while all $2(N-1)$ remaining views in the batch serve as negatives. Given L2-normalized projections $z_i$ and $z_j$ of a positive pair, the loss is $-\log \frac{\exp(\mathrm{sim}(z_i,z_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{k\neq i}\exp(\mathrm{sim}(z_i,z_k)/\tau)}$ with temperature $\tau=0.5$, averaged symmetrically over the two views. Pretraining runs for 200 epochs on the unlabeled training set, which we assume to be predominantly normal, without access to any anomaly labels.

At inference we discard the projection head and compute anomaly scores directly from the pretrained encoder. Following one-class deep SVDD, we first compute the mean embedding $c$ over the training set after pretraining, then score a test graph $G$ by the squared Euclidean distance $s(G)=\lVert f(G)-c\rVert_2^2$, where $f$ is the frozen encoder readout. Higher scores indicate anomalies; we calibrate a decision threshold on a held-out validation split using the 95th percentile of training scores.
