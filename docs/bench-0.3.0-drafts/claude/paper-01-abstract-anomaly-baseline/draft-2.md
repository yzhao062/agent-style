Graph-level anomaly detection—identifying graphs whose structure or attributes deviate from a normal population—is central to applications ranging from drug discovery to fraud analytics, yet labeled anomalies are typically scarce, heterogeneous, and expensive to curate. Existing unsupervised approaches either rely on reconstruction objectives that over-smooth subtle structural outliers, or on one-class boundaries that collapse when the normal distribution is multi-modal. We propose CGAD, a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework that learns graph-level representations by aligning two complementary views—a node-attribute-masked view and a subgraph-dropped view—under a hierarchical contrastive objective that couples node-, subgraph-, and graph-level agreement, and scores anomalies through a density-aware distance in the learned embedding space. The hierarchical coupling acts as a structural regularizer, preventing the encoder from discarding locally anomalous signal when optimizing a purely graph-level loss. Across six standard benchmarks from the TUDataset suite and two molecular datasets from OGB, CGAD improves mean AUC over the strongest prior unsupervised baseline by 4.7 points on PROTEINS and DD, by 6.2 points on ogbg-molhiv, and by 3.1 points averaged across IMDB-BINARY, REDDIT-BINARY, and COLLAB. Ablations show that removing the subgraph-level contrastive term accounts for the majority of the degradation, and that CGAD remains within 1 AUC point of its peak performance when the anomaly contamination rate in the unlabeled training set is increased from 0% to 15%. Code and pretrained encoders will be released.
