Detecting anomalous graphs—outliers within a collection of graphs—is central to applications ranging from molecular property screening to financial fraud analysis and cybersecurity triage. Existing approaches either depend on scarce anomaly labels or rely on reconstruction objectives that entangle structural complexity with anomalousness, leading to unstable performance across domains. We propose CGAD, a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework that learns graph-level embeddings by aligning augmented views of presumed-normal graphs under topology- and attribute-preserving perturbations, while enforcing a compactness prior that concentrates the normal class in representation space; anomaly scores combine k-nearest-neighbor distance on the learned manifold with a contrastive energy term. We evaluate CGAD on fourteen benchmarks spanning TU, OGB, and social graph collections. First, on TU datasets (DD, PROTEINS, NCI1, IMDB-BINARY), CGAD improves mean AUC by 4.8 points over the strongest prior unsupervised baseline (GOOD-D) and by 7.2 points over graph-kernel OCSVM. Second, on OGB molecular anomaly tasks (ogbg-molbace, ogbg-molhiv), it raises AUPRC by 6.3% while using 40% fewer pretraining epochs than InfoGraph-AD. Third, controlled ablations across five seeds attribute 3.1 AUC points to contrastive pretraining and an additional 1.9 points to the compactness regularizer, with gains robust to augmentation strength and backbone choice (GIN, GCN, GraphSAGE). Together, these results suggest that carefully designed contrastive objectives close much of the gap between unsupervised and semi-supervised graph-anomaly detection without requiring any labels at training time.
