## Methods

The encoder is a five-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) with 128-dimensional hidden representations and sum aggregation, followed by mean readout to produce a single graph embedding. Each GIN layer applies a two-layer multilayer perceptron with batch normalization and ReLU activations. Input node features are projected to the hidden dimension by a learned linear layer before the first message-passing step, and edge attributes, where present, are incorporated through the GINE variant. Residual connections link consecutive layers to stabilize training on deeper graphs, and dropout of 0.2 is applied to the readout vector. The choice of GIN reflects its provable equivalence to the Weisfeiler-Lehman test on graph isomorphism, which supplies discriminative power adequate for distinguishing structural anomalies from normal patterns.

For contrastive pretraining, we adopt a SimCLR-style objective adapted to graph data. Two stochastic augmentations generate a positive pair from each input graph: edge dropping with probability 0.15 and node-feature masking with probability 0.20. Negative samples come from the remaining graphs in the same mini-batch, yielding 2N − 2 negatives per anchor for batch size N. The InfoNCE loss with temperature τ = 0.5 maximizes agreement between positive pairs while separating embeddings of distinct graphs. We optimize using Adam with learning rate 1 × 10⁻³, batch size 256, and weight decay 1 × 10⁻⁵, training for 200 epochs on the assumed-clean training partition. A linear warmup over the first ten epochs precedes cosine decay of the learning rate.

At inference, the pretrained encoder produces a fixed-dimensional embedding for every test graph. We define the anomaly score as the cosine distance between a test embedding and the mean embedding of the training set, computed once at the end of pretraining. Higher cosine distance indicates greater deviation from the typical pattern learned during contrastive training, under the assumption that anomalous graphs occupy peripheral regions of the learned representation space. To improve robustness, we use a k-nearest-neighbor variant in which the score is the average cosine distance to the k = 5 closest training embeddings, which dampens spurious drift from within-class heterogeneity. No labeled anomalies enter any stage of training or score calibration.
