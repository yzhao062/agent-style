## Methods

The pipeline has three stages: a graph encoder that produces fixed-dimensional embeddings, a contrastive objective that shapes the embedding space without labels, and a scoring rule that flags anomalies at test time.

The encoder is a five-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) with hidden dimension 128 and ReLU activations. Each layer updates a node's representation by summing its neighbors' features and passing the result through a two-layer multilayer perceptron, following the aggregation scheme that separates non-isomorphic graphs under mild assumptions. Sum-pooled readouts from every layer concatenate into a 640-dimensional graph embedding, which a two-layer projection head maps into a 128-dimensional space where the contrastive loss operates. Training uses AdamW at learning rate 1e-3 and weight decay 1e-5.

For pretraining, we form two views of each input graph by independently applying node dropping at rate 0.2 and edge perturbation (random addition and removal of 10 percent of edges); the two views make a positive pair. Negatives come from the remaining graphs in the same mini-batch, which holds 128 graphs per step and yields 254 negatives for every positive. We optimize the normalized temperature-scaled cross-entropy loss (NT-Xent) with temperature 0.2, which stabilized gradients in our runs relative to the common default of 0.5. Pretraining covers 100 epochs over a training split that contains only putatively normal graphs.

At inference, a test graph receives a score equal to its average cosine distance to the k nearest training embeddings in the projection space, with k = 10. Graphs that sit far from the dense region of normal samples receive high scores, and a threshold calibrated on a held-out validation split converts scores into binary anomaly labels. The nearest-neighbor rule needs no parametric density estimate and remains robust when the normal class splits into several modes, a pattern that appeared on the molecular and protein datasets used in our evaluation. Because the encoder weights stay fixed after pretraining, scoring a new graph requires one forward pass plus a k-nearest-neighbor lookup, which keeps inference latency below ten milliseconds per graph on a single V100.
