## Experiments

We evaluated the proposed model on five widely used node-level graph anomaly benchmarks spanning citation, social, and transaction-like domains. Cora is a citation network in which papers are linked by references and anomalies correspond to papers whose attributes are inconsistent with their citation neighborhoods. Citeseer has the same citation-graph structure but is sparser, making it useful for testing robustness when neighborhood evidence is weak. PubMed is a larger biomedical citation graph with more homogeneous node features, so successful detection depends on subtle structural deviations rather than obvious attribute outliers. BlogCatalog is a social network of bloggers with profile features, where anomalies reflect users whose stated interests do not align with their social communities. Flickr is a large image-sharing social graph in which node attributes describe user tags and activity patterns, producing anomalies that mix community drift with attribute irregularity.

For each dataset, we used the standard transductive setting: the full graph structure and node attributes were visible during training, while anomaly labels were hidden except for validation and final evaluation. Nodes were split into 60% training, 20% validation, and 20% test sets with stratification by anomaly label; training used only unlabeled node features and adjacency information, validation labels were used for early stopping and hyperparameter selection, and all reported numbers were computed on the held-out test split. Because anomalies are rare and class imbalance varies across datasets, we report AUROC as the primary metric and AUPRC as a secondary metric. Each experiment was repeated over ten random seeds controlling split generation, parameter initialization, and mini-batch sampling, and we report mean performance across seeds.

The proposed method achieved the best average AUROC across all five datasets, with 91.8% AUROC and 63.4% AUPRC, compared with 84.7%/51.2% for DOMINANT and 87.3%/55.9% for AnomalyDAE. Gains were largest on sparse citation graphs: on Citeseer, our model reached 90.6% AUROC, improving over DOMINANT by 8.9 points and over CoLA by 5.1 points. On the larger Flickr benchmark, performance remained stable at 92.4% AUROC, while CONAD and SL-GAD obtained 88.0% and 89.1%, respectively. The strongest ablation removed the structural-context contrastive objective, reducing average AUROC from 91.8% to 88.1% and AUPRC from 63.4% to 57.0%, indicating that jointly modeling attribute reconstruction and neighborhood-consistency signals is essential for separating true anomalies from merely low-degree or noisy nodes.
