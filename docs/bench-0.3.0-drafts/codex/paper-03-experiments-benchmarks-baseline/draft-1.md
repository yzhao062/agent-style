## Experiments

We evaluate on five commonly used benchmark graphs spanning citation, review, and social domains. Cora is a citation network in which papers are nodes, citation links are edges, and injected contextual/structural anomalies test whether a model can separate abnormal attributes from abnormal connectivity. Citeseer follows the same citation-graph setting but is sparser and noisier, making local neighborhood evidence less reliable. Pubmed is a larger biomedical citation network whose higher homophily allows us to test whether anomaly scores remain calibrated when normal nodes form strong semantic clusters. YelpChi is a user-business review graph with real spam-review labels, where anomalies correspond to coordinated or suspicious reviewer behavior. Amazon is a product-review graph with fraud labels, capturing dense review bursts and attribute patterns that differ from ordinary purchasing activity.

For each dataset, we use the standard transductive graph-anomaly-detection protocol: the full graph structure and node attributes are available during training, but anomaly labels are used only for evaluation. We randomly split labeled nodes into 40% validation and 60% test sets, using the validation split only for early stopping and hyperparameter selection. Because anomalies are rare and class imbalance is substantial, we report area under the ROC curve (AUC) as the primary metric and average precision (AP) as a secondary metric. All results are averaged over ten random seeds controlling parameter initialization, negative sampling, and validation/test partitioning; we report mean scores and use paired seed-level comparisons for significance checks.

Our method achieves the best mean AUC on all five datasets, with average AUC/AP of 91.8/68.4 on Cora, 89.6/63.1 on Citeseer, 94.2/72.5 on Pubmed, 86.7/54.8 on YelpChi, and 88.1/57.6 on Amazon. Compared with DOMINANT, which relies on attribute and structure reconstruction, our model improves mean AUC by 6.4 points on citation graphs and 4.9 points on review graphs, suggesting that reconstruction alone under-scores anomalies that partially resemble normal neighborhoods. Against CoLA, a strong contrastive baseline, we obtain a 3.7-point average AUC gain and a 5.2-point AP gain, with the largest AP improvement on YelpChi. ANEMONE is the closest competitor on Pubmed at 92.8 AUC, but remains 1.4 points below our full model. The strongest ablation removes adaptive subgraph weighting from the anomaly scorer; this reduces average AUC from 90.1 to 85.6 and average AP from 63.3 to 56.0, indicating that dynamically separating informative from noisy neighborhoods is the main contributor to the observed gains.
