Graph-level anomaly detection aims to identify rare or abnormal graphs when only unlabeled collections are available, a setting common in molecular screening, fraud analysis, and system monitoring. Existing unsupervised methods often rely on reconstruction losses or hand-designed graph statistics, which can reward low-level fidelity while missing semantic deviations in structure and attributes. We propose GraphCLAD, a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework for graph-level anomaly detection. GraphCLAD learns an encoder by bringing two semantics-preserving views of the same graph together while separating views from other graphs, then scores anomalies by the consistency of each graph with a compact normality model in the learned representation space. The pretraining objective combines topology-aware edge perturbations, attribute masking, and subgraph sampling with a hardness-weighted contrastive loss, so the encoder captures stable graph-level semantics rather than augmentation artifacts. Across eight benchmark datasets, GraphCLAD improves mean AUROC by 6.3 points over the strongest unsupervised baseline. On AIDS and DHFR, it raises AUROC from 0.873 to 0.926 and from 0.741 to 0.806, respectively, while using no anomaly labels for model selection. On the social-network datasets REDDIT-BINARY and IMDB-MULTI, GraphCLAD improves AUPRC by 4.8 and 5.5 points, showing that contrastive pretraining transfers beyond molecular graphs. Ablations show that removing hardness weighting reduces mean AUROC by 3.1 points, indicating that contrastive pair selection is central to robust graph-level anomaly detection.
