Graph-level anomaly detection seeks to identify rare graphs whose structure or attributes deviate from a normal population, without access to labeled anomalies during training. This setting arises in molecular screening, fraud analysis, and system monitoring, where anomalous graphs are scarce, heterogeneous, and expensive to label. We propose GraphCLAD, a contrastive self-supervised pretraining method that learns normality-sensitive graph representations by contrasting each graph with structure-preserving augmentations while pushing apart representations induced by anomaly-seeking perturbations. The pretrained encoder is then fit with a one-class density objective, so anomaly scores reflect both semantic mismatch and low support under the learned normal distribution.

Across 12 graph-level anomaly benchmarks, GraphCLAD improves mean AUROC by 5.8 percentage points over the strongest unsupervised baseline and by 9.6 points over reconstruction-based graph autoencoders. On molecular datasets, it raises AUROC from 83.4 to 91.7 on AIDS and from 76.2 to 82.5 on BZR, suggesting that contrastive pretraining captures substructure changes missed by node-reconstruction losses. On social graph benchmarks, GraphCLAD improves AUPRC by 6.3 points on IMDB-BINARY and 4.9 points on REDDIT-BINARY when anomalies are injected by motif rewiring at a 5% contamination rate. Ablations show that anomaly-seeking negative perturbations account for 3.1 AUROC points on average, while the one-class scoring head adds 2.4 points over nearest-neighbor scoring. These results indicate that contrastive pretraining provides a robust foundation for unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection.
