### Specific Aim 1: Adaptive Outlier Detection for Nonstationary Clinical Streams

Streaming clinical data such as vital signs, device telemetry, laboratory results, and medication events contain rare but consequential outliers caused by sensor faults, documentation artifacts, abrupt physiology, and care-process changes. The research question is: how can an outlier detector update fast enough to track patient- and unit-level distribution shift while preserving statistical control over false alarms in high-dimensional, irregular clinical streams? This aim will develop and evaluate an adaptive detection framework that separates transient anomalies from durable regime changes and reports calibrated uncertainty with each alert.

The novelty is a drift-aware detector that couples online representation learning, conformal calibration, and clinically constrained forgetting. Prior approaches often fix a baseline window, retrain after drift is detected, or optimize anomaly scores without prospective error control. Our approach treats adaptation, calibration, and clinical plausibility as one learning problem: the detector will update latent representations continuously, revise thresholds under conformal validity guarantees, and restrict forgetting when changes conflict with physiological or workflow constraints.

Task 1 will define the streaming data model and adaptive detector. We will implement online embeddings for mixed-rate, partially observed clinical variables and compare bounded-memory update rules. Milestones are: processing at least 10,000 events per second on a single workstation, less than 5% degradation in area under the precision-recall curve under synthetic missingness, and documented bounds on memory growth.

Task 2 will develop calibration and drift handling. We will design conformal score updates that distinguish isolated outliers from sustained shifts in measurement practice or patient state. Milestones are: empirical false-alarm rates within two percentage points of the target level across simulated drift scenarios, detection of abrupt drift within 100 events, and stable operation under gradual drift over 30-day replay streams.

Task 3 will evaluate clinical streaming performance. We will test the method on retrospective intensive-care and perioperative streams with timestamp-preserving replay, clinician-adjudicated subsets, and ablation studies. Milestones are: at least 20% higher precision at matched recall than nonadaptive baselines, latency below one second per event batch, and a reproducible benchmark package with fixed train, calibration, and test splits.

The intellectual merit rests on scientific rigor, investigator qualifications, and feasibility. Rigor comes from formal calibration targets, controlled drift experiments, preregistered evaluation metrics, and ablations that isolate each algorithmic component. The investigators combine expertise in streaming algorithms, machine learning, clinical informatics, and hospital data engineering. Feasibility follows from available retrospective streams, existing secure compute infrastructure, and a staged plan that yields testable artifacts after each task.
