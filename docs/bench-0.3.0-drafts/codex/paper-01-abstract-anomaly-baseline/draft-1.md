Detecting anomalous graphs is central to applications such as molecule screening, fraud analysis, and system monitoring, yet labels are rare and anomalies are often defined only relative to a heterogeneous population of normal graphs. Existing graph-level detectors typically rely on handcrafted statistics, reconstruction objectives, or supervised validation signals, which can fail when anomalous graphs preserve local motifs while differing in higher-order organization. We propose a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework that learns graph representations by aligning multiple semantics-preserving views of the same graph while explicitly separating views drawn from different graphs. The key idea is to couple structure-aware augmentations with a prototype-regularized contrastive objective, producing embeddings in which normal graphs form compact, transferable clusters and anomalies are scored by their distance to the learned normal manifold.

Across eight unsupervised graph anomaly benchmarks, the method improves mean AUROC from 78.6 to 84.1, a 5.5-point gain over the strongest prior self-supervised baseline. On molecular datasets, it raises AUROC by 6.8 points on AIDS and 4.9 points on BZR, with the largest gains observed for anomalies characterized by subtle substructure rearrangements rather than node-label shifts. On social and transaction graphs, it improves average precision by 7.3 points on IMDB-BINARY-Anom and reduces false positives at 95% recall by 18% on REDDIT-MULTI-5K-Anom. Ablations show that both prototype regularization and augmentation calibration are necessary, accounting for 3.1 and 2.4 AUROC points respectively.
