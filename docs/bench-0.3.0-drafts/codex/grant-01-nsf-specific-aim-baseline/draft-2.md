### Specific Aim 1: Drift-Aware Outlier Detection for Streaming Clinical Data

Streaming clinical data are nonstationary, heterogeneous, and sparsely labeled, making conventional outlier detection unreliable when patient state, sensor quality, and care context change over time. This aim asks: how can an online outlier detector distinguish clinically meaningful deviations from artifacts and benign distribution shifts while maintaining calibrated alert rates under latency, missingness, and label scarcity constraints? Prior streaming approaches typically assume fixed contamination levels, stationary reference distributions, or delayed batch recalibration; clinical early-warning models often optimize supervised prediction rather than statistically valid outlier evidence. The novelty of this aim is a drift-aware, uncertainty-calibrated detection framework that combines online representation learning, conformal risk control, and interpretable evidence summaries so thresholds adapt to evolving streams without suppressing rare but important physiological anomalies.

The work will proceed in three tasks. Task 1 will formalize streaming clinical outliers under distribution shift, including missingness, irregular sampling, and sensor artifacts. Milestones are: define a benchmark protocol on at least three de-identified clinical stream types, specify clinically plausible shift scenarios, and release evaluation metrics for detection delay, false-alert burden, calibration error, and robustness to missing data. Task 2 will develop adaptive detectors that update representations and thresholds online while preserving finite-sample uncertainty guarantees where assumptions permit. Milestones are: implement at least two algorithmic variants, demonstrate at least a 20% reduction in false alerts at matched sensitivity against fixed-threshold baselines, and maintain calibration error below a prespecified bound across simulated drift regimes. Task 3 will validate the methods in retrospective streaming replay and ablation studies. Milestones are: quantify detection delay relative to expert-labeled or proxy clinical events, test robustness across patient subgroups and care settings without using demographic framing as an objective, and produce interpretable summaries identifying the measurements and temporal patterns driving each alert.

This aim aligns with NSF intellectual merit criteria in three ways. Scientific rigor is addressed through formal problem definitions, controlled drift experiments, calibration analysis, and reproducible benchmarks rather than anecdotal model comparisons. Investigator qualifications support execution because the team’s combined expertise spans streaming algorithms, statistical machine learning, biomedical informatics, and clinical data governance. Feasibility is supported by a staged plan that begins with retrospective data and simulation, uses measurable performance gates before integration, and limits scope to algorithmic validity, robustness, and evaluation infrastructure within the proposed period.
