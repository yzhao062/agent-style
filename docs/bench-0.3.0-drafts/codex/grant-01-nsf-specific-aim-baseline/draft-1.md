### Specific Aim 1: Drift-Aware Outlier Detection for Streaming Clinical Signals

Streaming clinical data from bedside monitors, laboratory feeds, and EHR event logs contain artifacts, rare deteriorations, and abrupt practice-driven distribution shifts that defeat static outlier detectors. The research question is: how can an outlier detector update online to distinguish clinically meaningful anomalies from evolving normal care patterns while preserving statistical error control under nonstationary, partially labeled streams?

The novelty is an adaptive detection framework that couples conformal risk calibration, drift-localized representation learning, and clinician-auditable evidence summaries in one online algorithm. Prior approaches typically optimize retrospective anomaly scores, assume stationarity, or retrain in batches after drift has already degraded performance. This aim instead treats adaptation, uncertainty calibration, and explanation as joint streaming constraints, enabling bounded false-alarm behavior while the data distribution changes.

Task 1 will formalize the streaming detection problem and develop drift-sensitive representations for multivariate clinical time series with missingness, irregular sampling, and heterogeneous event types. Milestones are: a benchmark suite from at least three retrospective clinical streams; labeled artifact, physiologic, and workflow-shift cases; and baseline comparisons against isolation forest, autoencoder, density-ratio, and conformal detectors.

Task 2 will design the adaptive detection algorithm. The system will maintain local calibration sets, detect distributional change, and update decision thresholds without full retraining. Milestones are: detection latency below 10 streaming observations after simulated drift; false-alert inflation bounded within 5% of the target rate; and at least 15% improvement in area under the precision-recall curve over the strongest nonadaptive baseline.

Task 3 will evaluate robustness and interpretability under clinically realistic stressors, including sensor dropouts, delayed labels, unit-specific case mix, and changing measurement frequency. Milestones are: reproducible ablation studies, uncertainty-calibrated alerts with feature-time evidence summaries, and prospective replay experiments showing stable performance across at least two temporal holdout periods.

This aim aligns with NSF intellectual merit by advancing scientific rigor through formal error criteria, controlled drift experiments, preregistered metrics, and reproducible open-source evaluation code. The investigators are qualified through complementary expertise in streaming machine learning, clinical informatics, statistical calibration, and health-data systems. Feasibility is supported by existing access to retrospective clinical streams, established baseline implementations, and a staged plan in which each task produces measurable artifacts that directly enable the next.
