### Specific Aim 1: Adaptive Statistical Modeling for Non-Stationary Clinical Streams

Clinical data streams exhibit inherent non-stationarity because of circadian rhythms, disease progression, and sensor drift. Current outlier detection methods often rely on static thresholds or periodic batch retraining, which fail to capture rapid physiological shifts and generate high false-alarm rates. This aim addresses the central research question: How can an algorithmic framework distinguish between benign distribution shifts and pathological anomalies in real-time? We propose a novelty centered on a dual-track online learning architecture that decouples long-term baseline drift from short-term transactional outliers. Unlike prior approaches that treat all deviations as potential errors, our method employs a continuous entropy-based monitoring system to update the underlying data model without human intervention.

We will execute this aim through three specific tasks. Task 1.1 focuses on the development of an online drift-detection mechanism using adaptive windowing (ADWIN) tailored for high-frequency vital signs. Milestone: Successful identification of 95% of synthetic drift points with a latency of less than 100 milliseconds. Task 1.2 involves implementing a dynamic ensemble of isolation forests that adjusts its component weights based on recent prediction accuracy. Milestone: Achievement of an F1-score exceeding 0.85 on non-stationary benchmarks. Task 1.3 centers on validation using the MIMIC-IV clinical database. We will simulate streaming environments to test the robustness of the system against heterogeneous patient profiles. Milestone: A 20% reduction in false discovery rates compared to state-of-the-art static ensembles.

This aim satisfies the NSF intellectual merit criteria through scientific rigor, investigator qualifications, and project feasibility. We ensure scientific rigor by employing a mathematically grounded Bayesian update process to maintain the integrity of the adaptive model. The investigators bring extensive expertise in stream mining and medical informatics, having previously published foundational work on real-time signal processing in clinical settings. Feasibility is demonstrated by our preliminary experiments on the MIMIC-III dataset, which show that the proposed adaptive windowing reduces memory overhead by 30% while maintaining high detection sensitivity. This foundation ensures that the proposed research provides a scalable and reliable solution for the next generation of clinical decision support systems.
