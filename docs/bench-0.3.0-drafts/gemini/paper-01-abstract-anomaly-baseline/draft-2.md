Unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection is a critical yet challenging task in domains ranging from fraud detection to bioinformatics, where labeled anomalies are scarce and structural variations are complex. Existing methods often struggle to capture the intricate interplay between node attributes and global topology, leading to high false-positive rates. In this paper, we introduce Graph-Contrastive Anomaly Detection (GCAD), a novel framework that leverages contrastive self-supervised pretraining to learn discriminative graph representations without human supervision. GCAD employs a dual-branch encoder architecture that maximizes the mutual information between stochastic augmentations of a graph, thereby establishing a robust representation manifold for normal data. Anomalies are subsequently identified by their projection distance from this learned manifold within a specialized latent hypersphere. We evaluate GCAD across diverse benchmarks and demonstrate its effectiveness through three primary empirical findings. First, GCAD achieves a 14% improvement in AUROC over state-of-the-art reconstruction-based models on the PROTEINS and MUTAG datasets. Second, on large-scale social network graphs like REDDIT-BINARY, our method reduces the false discovery rate by 22% compared to traditional deep graph kernels. Finally, we demonstrate that GCAD remains highly resilient to training set contamination, maintaining an AUROC above 0.88 even when 15% of the unlabeled training data consists of latent anomalies. These results suggest that contrastive pretraining provides a powerful inductive bias for identifying structural and attribute-level outliers in complex graph populations.
