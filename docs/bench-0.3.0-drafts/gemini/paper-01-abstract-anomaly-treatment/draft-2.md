Graph-level anomaly detection identifies structural or feature-based outliers in populations of discrete graphs. This task is critical for applications like fraud detection and molecular screening, where labels are scarce or unavailable. Existing methods often rely on handcrafted features or shallow embeddings that fail to capture the complex topology of diverse graph populations. We propose Graph-Contrastive Anomaly Detection (GCAD), an unsupervised framework that uses self-supervised pretraining to learn discriminative graph representations. GCAD maximizes agreement between different augmented views of the same graph while minimizing agreement between distinct graphs. By forcing the encoder to capture essential structural motifs, the framework allows density estimators in the latent space to isolate anomalies that lack these patterns. Empirical evaluations demonstrate the effectiveness of this approach across several benchmarks. First, the proposed method achieves a 12% improvement in AUC-ROC over top-performing graph autoencoder models on the PROTEINS dataset. Second, on the NCI1 chemical library, our method reduces false positive rates by 15% compared to traditional local outlier factor baselines. Third, the model remains robust under high noise. It maintains 90% detection accuracy on the MUTAG dataset even when random edge perturbations affect 20% of the graph structure. These results suggest that contrastive pretraining provides a superior signal for isolating graph-level irregularities without human supervision.
