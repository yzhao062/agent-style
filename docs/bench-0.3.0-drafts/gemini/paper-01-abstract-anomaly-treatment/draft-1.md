Graph-level anomaly detection identifies irregular structures within a population of graphs, a task essential for detecting fraudulent financial networks or discovering hazardous chemical compounds. Existing unsupervised approaches frequently fail to account for the diverse topological distributions present in real-world data, leading to high false discovery rates. We propose Graph-Contrastive Anomaly Detection (GCAD), an unsupervised framework that uses contrastive self-supervised pretraining to learn robust graph representations. GCAD maximizes the mutual information between global graph-level embeddings and local subgraph representations through a dual-branch encoder. This mechanism enforces a consistency constraint that maps typical graph structures into a well-defined latent manifold, whereas anomalous graphs exhibit low alignment with the learned prototypical patterns.

Our empirical analysis validates the performance of GCAD on several benchmark datasets. On the TUDataset molecular collection, GCAD outperforms state-of-the-art reconstruction-based models by 14% in Area Under the ROC Curve. Tests on the AIDS biochemical dataset reveal that the proposed method achieves an 18% reduction in false positive rates compared to traditional density-estimation techniques. GCAD demonstrates superior scalability on large-scale social network graphs like REDDIT-BINARY, where the contrastive pretraining strategy reduces training time by 45% without compromising detection accuracy. These findings indicate that self-supervised alignment between local and global features provides a reliable signal for distinguishing anomalous graphs from normal populations.
