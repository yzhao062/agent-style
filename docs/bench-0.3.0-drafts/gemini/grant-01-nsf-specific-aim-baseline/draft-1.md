### Specific Aim 1: Dynamic Latent Distribution Modeling for Drift-Resilient Outlier Detection

The central research question for this aim is: *How can we mathematically distinguish between stochastic clinical anomalies and benign non-stationary shifts in high-velocity physiological data streams?* Existing outlier detection paradigms in clinical informatics typically rely on static thresholds or batch-processed moving averages. These approaches fail in real-time streaming contexts because they cannot differentiate between "true" physiological emergencies and "concept drift"—the gradual or sudden shifts in data distribution caused by sensor degradation, lead displacement, or normal circadian variability. The novelty of our approach lies in the development of a recursive Bayesian updating framework that utilizes non-stationary kernels to decouple drift from outliers. Unlike prior methods that require periodic retraining, our model adapts in-situ, treating distribution shifts as a predictable latent process to isolate anomalies with unprecedented resolution.

This research will be realized through three technical tasks:
1. **Task 1: Development of the Adaptive Kernel Density Estimation (AKDE) framework.** We will derive a recursive density estimator that dynamically adjusts its bandwidth according to local stream variance. *Milestone:* Validation of the AKDE framework achieving a false discovery rate of <5% on the PhysioNet MIMIC-III streaming database.
2. **Task 2: Integration of Change-Point Detection (CPD) for Drift Attribution.** We will implement a CUSUM-based detection layer to classify distribution shifts into "sensor-driven" or "pathophysiological" categories. *Milestone:* Successful attribution of >90% of simulated drift events in a controlled synthetic environment.
3. **Task 3: Real-Time Benchmarking and Complexity Analysis.** We will optimize the algorithm for low-power edge devices using a sliding-window forest structure. *Milestone:* Demonstrating a 25% improvement in detection latency and a 15% reduction in memory overhead compared to the current industry-standard Robust Random Cut Forest (RRCF) algorithm.

The **Intellectual Merit** of this aim is substantiated by its scientific rigor, as the AKDE framework will be accompanied by formal mathematical proofs of convergence under non-stationary conditions. The feasibility is supported by the PI’s documented expertise in real-time signal processing and existing access to high-fidelity clinical datasets. By establishing a rigorous foundation for drift-resilient detection, this aim provides the necessary computational infrastructure for reliable clinical decision support.
