### Specific Aim 1: Drift-Aware Online Learning for Robust Clinical Stream Monitoring

The primary challenge in real-time clinical monitoring is the accurate differentiation between acute physiological crises and artifact-driven noise within non-stationary data streams. Aim 1 addresses the fundamental research question: *How can we architect an online outlier detection framework that maintains high sensitivity while autonomously adapting to the evolving statistical properties of high-frequency physiological data?* Current clinical decision support systems typically rely on static thresholds or periodic batch-retraining. Such methods are fundamentally ill-equipped to handle "concept drift"—the shift in data distributions caused by changes in patient pathology, medication administration, or sensor calibration. This research introduces a novel, drift-aware streaming ensemble that utilizes online kernel density estimation and lightweight structural updates, providing a continuous, high-fidelity assessment of patient stability without requiring manual intervention.

The execution of this aim involves three interrelated tasks:

**Task 1.1: Dynamic Feature Extraction for Multi-Modal Streams.** We will design a recursive dimensionality reduction algorithm tailored for concurrent clinical streams, including ECG, SpO2, and arterial pressure. This task focuses on preserving essential physiological signals while filtering high-frequency sensor noise. **Milestone:** Real-time extraction of salient features with a processing latency of less than 5 milliseconds per observation on commodity hardware.

**Task 1.2: Development of an Adaptive Ensemble Architecture.** We will implement an ensemble of streaming half-space trees that utilizes a competitive weighting mechanism to prioritize sub-models most consistent with recent data windows. This approach allows the system to maintain a "memory" of historical patterns while prioritizing current patient states. **Milestone:** Achievement of an AUC-ROC exceeding 0.90 on the MIMIC-III Waveform Database, demonstrating superior performance over traditional batch-processed isolation forests.

**Task 1.3: Drift-Responsive Model Refinement.** We will integrate a Page-Hinkley based change-point detection module to identify distribution shifts and trigger localized model updates rather than global retraining. **Milestone:** A 40% reduction in false alarm rates during simulated hemodynamic shifts compared to non-adaptive baselines.

This aim fulfills the NSF **Intellectual Merit** criteria by advancing the state of the art in online learning within the high-stakes domain of healthcare. The project demonstrates **scientific rigor** through the application of robust statistical process control to stochastic clinical environments. The **feasibility** is evidenced by our preliminary work with the eICU Collaborative Research Database, and the **qualifications of the investigators**, who possess significant experience in both signal processing and clinical informatics, ensure the successful execution of these technical milestones.
