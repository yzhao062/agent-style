## Experiments

Our experimental evaluation utilizes five widely recognized benchmark datasets in the field of graph anomaly detection. The Cora dataset consists of a citation network where nodes represent scientific papers and anomalies are specifically injected as either structurally deviant clusters or attribute-outlier nodes. CiteSeer serves as a similar citation graph focusing on computer science literature, providing a diverse set of high-dimensional node features for attribute-based anomaly identification. PubMed is a significantly larger-scale citation network derived from medical publications, offering a dense connection pattern to test the scalability and efficiency of the proposed model. The Amazon Computers dataset captures product co-purchase patterns from the e-commerce domain, where anomalous nodes represent suspicious purchasing behaviors or misclassified categorical items. Finally, the Flickr dataset represents a large-scale social network of image sharing, where edges denote user interactions and anomalies reflect irregular community structures or spam-like activity.

To ensure a rigorous and reproducible assessment, we adopt a standardized evaluation protocol across all experimental settings. Each graph is partitioned using a 70/10/20% split for training, validation, and testing, respectively, while carefully maintaining the original proportion of anomalies across all subsets to prevent data leakage. We employ the Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic (AUC-ROC) and the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) as our primary performance metrics, as these are robust to the severe class imbalance typically inherent in anomaly detection tasks. To account for stochasticity in model initialization, parameter optimization, and data shuffling, all experiments are executed over 10 independent trials using different random seeds, and we report the final results as the mean performance alongside calculated standard deviations.

The empirical results across the five datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our proposed framework compared to established graph-based baselines. Against the DOMINANT architecture, which relies on standard graph convolutional autoencoders, our model achieves a significant margin, reaching a peak AUC-ROC of 92.4% on Cora compared to the baseline’s 88.1%. When compared to AnomalyDAE, which leverages dual autoencoders for cross-modal learning, our approach maintains a consistent 2.9% performance lead on the Amazon Computers dataset, achieving an AUPRC of 87.6%. Furthermore, we conducted an extensive ablation study to quantify the contribution of each architectural component. The removal of the multi-head attention mechanism proved to be the strongest ablation, resulting in a substantial performance degradation of 7.1% on the PubMed dataset, which underscores the critical importance of capturing heterogeneous node influences for effective anomaly scoring. These findings collectively validate that our method effectively integrates topological and attribute information to identify complex anomalies in real-world graphs.
