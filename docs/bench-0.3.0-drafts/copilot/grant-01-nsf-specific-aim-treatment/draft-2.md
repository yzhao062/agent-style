### Specific Aim 1: Drift-Resilient Outlier Detection for Streaming Clinical Data

This aim asks: how can an outlier detector operating on streaming clinical data identify clinically consequential anomalies in real time while adapting to patient-specific baselines, sensor artifacts, and distribution shift across care contexts? We hypothesize that outlier detection will be more accurate and more stable if adaptation, uncertainty estimation, and drift detection are learned jointly rather than handled by separate thresholding and post hoc recalibration steps.

The novelty of this aim is a unified online framework for adaptive outlier detection that couples temporal representation learning with explicit shift monitoring and calibrated anomaly scores. Prior methods for clinical anomaly detection commonly assume fixed thresholds, train batch models that degrade under nonstationarity, or adapt aggressively in ways that absorb rare but important events into the new baseline. Our approach will instead distinguish physiological change from measurement corruption and population drift through continual updating constrained by uncertainty and change-point evidence. This combination moves beyond prior approaches by making adaptation selective, interpretable, and measurable under streaming conditions.

The work proceeds in three tasks. Task 1 will define the problem formally, assemble multimodal retrospective streams, and create a benchmark covering vital signs, laboratory updates, and device data. Milestone: a reproducible benchmark with labeled anomaly categories and prespecified metrics for false-alarm rate, detection delay, calibration error, and performance under simulated drift. Task 2 will develop adaptive algorithms that integrate online sequence modeling, patient-context conditioning, and drift-aware score calibration. Milestone: at least a 20% reduction in false alarms relative to strong static and batch-adaptive baselines while maintaining bounded detection delay. Task 3 will test robustness under missingness, irregular sampling, and transfer across hospital units. Milestone: statistically significant gains on at least three stream types and release of a reproducible prototype.

This aim aligns with NSF intellectual-merit criteria. Scientific rigor will come from explicit hypotheses, ablation studies, calibrated uncertainty analysis, and prespecified statistical comparisons. The investigators are well qualified because the team combines expertise in machine learning, streaming systems, and clinical data analysis. Feasibility is strong because the plan is staged, the milestones are measurable, and the work relies on available retrospective datasets and established compute infrastructure.
