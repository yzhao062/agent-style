### Specific Aim 1: Adaptive Outlier Detection for Nonstationary Clinical Streams

**Research question:** How can an outlier-detection system distinguish clinically meaningful anomalies from sensor noise, workflow artifacts, and population drift in streaming clinical data while preserving low latency and calibrated uncertainty? This aim will develop a principled framework for adaptive outlier detection over multimodal clinical streams, including vital signs, laboratory updates, and device-generated measurements, where the data distribution changes across patients, care settings, and time.

The central novelty is an online detection architecture that jointly models temporal dependence, patient-specific context, and distribution shift. Prior approaches typically rely on static thresholds, batch-trained anomaly models, or adaptation rules that treat drift and outliers as separate problems. In contrast, this project will unify drift-aware updating with uncertainty-calibrated scoring so that the detector can adapt when baseline physiology changes, yet resist contamination from transient artifacts and rare pathological events. The result is a more scientifically grounded detector that is responsive to nonstationary streams without erasing the very signals clinicians need to see.

The work is organized into three tasks with measurable milestones. **Task 1** will formalize the streaming setting, define a taxonomy of outlier types, and construct benchmark pipelines from retrospective clinical streams. Milestone: a reproducible benchmark suite with labeled event categories, latency constraints, and evaluation metrics for detection delay, false-alarm rate, calibration error, and robustness under simulated drift. **Task 2** will design adaptive algorithms that combine online representation learning, change-point monitoring, and confidence-aware scoring. Milestone: methods that improve false-alarm rate by at least 20% relative to strong static and batch-adaptive baselines while maintaining bounded detection delay on benchmark tasks. **Task 3** will validate the methods under realistic deployment conditions, including missingness, asynchronous sampling, and cross-unit transfer. Milestone: statistically significant gains in robustness across at least three clinical stream types and an open-source prototype with reproducible evaluation scripts.

This aim aligns directly with NSF intellectual merit. **Scientific rigor** is ensured through explicit hypotheses, ablation studies, uncertainty calibration, and pre-specified evaluation criteria. **Qualifications of the investigators** are reflected in the team’s combined expertise in machine learning, streaming systems, and clinical data analysis, which is necessary to connect theory, algorithms, and realistic datasets. **Feasibility** is supported by the staged task structure, measurable milestones, and reliance on available retrospective data and established computational infrastructure.
