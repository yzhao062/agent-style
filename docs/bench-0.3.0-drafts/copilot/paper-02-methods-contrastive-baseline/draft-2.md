## Methods

We model each input graph \(G=(V,E,X)\) with a graph encoder designed to capture both local topology and graph-level semantics before anomaly scoring. The encoder is a 3-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN), where each message-passing layer is followed by batch normalization and ReLU activation. Hidden representations have dimension 128, and dropout with rate 0.2 is applied after each layer to reduce overfitting. Node embeddings from all layers are aggregated by sum pooling and concatenated, then projected by a 2-layer multilayer perceptron to a 128-dimensional graph embedding \(z\). During pretraining, the encoder parameters are optimized with Adam using a learning rate of \(1\times10^{-3}\) and weight decay of \(1\times10^{-5}\).

The encoder is pretrained with a contrastive self-supervised objective so that normal graphs occupy a compact region of representation space without requiring anomaly labels. For each graph, we generate two correlated views using stochastic graph augmentations: node-feature masking with probability 0.1, edge dropping with probability 0.15, and random-walk subgraph sampling that retains 90% of nodes. These two views form a positive pair because they preserve the underlying graph identity while perturbing superficial structure. Negative pairs are constructed implicitly from other graphs in the same mini-batch, following the standard in-batch contrastive setting; with batch size 128, each anchor has one positive and 254 negatives. Let \(z_i\) and \(z_i^+\) denote embeddings of the two augmented views. We minimize the normalized temperature-scaled cross-entropy loss (InfoNCE), using cosine similarity and temperature \(\tau=0.2\), encouraging agreement between positives and separation from negatives. Pretraining is run for 200 epochs with early stopping based on validation stability of the contrastive loss.

At inference, anomaly detection is performed directly on the pretrained embedding space. We first compute a reference prototype \(\mu\) as the mean embedding over the training graphs, which are assumed to be predominantly normal. For a test graph, we produce its embedding from the unaugmented graph and calculate an anomaly score as the sum of two terms: the squared Euclidean distance to the prototype, \(\|z-\mu\|_2^2\), and an instability penalty measuring disagreement between the embeddings of 5 independently augmented views of the same graph. The final score is \(s(G)=\|z-\mu\|_2^2 + 0.5\,\mathrm{Var}(\{z^{(m)}\}_{m=1}^5)\). Graphs with large scores are deemed anomalous because they are both far from the learned normal manifold and insensitive less consistently to structure-preserving perturbations.
