### Specific Aim 1: Adaptive Outlier Detection for Nonstationary Clinical Streams

This aim asks: **How can outlier detection remain accurate, calibrated, and clinically interpretable when streaming clinical data exhibit drift, missingness, irregular sampling, and shifting patient baselines?** Current methods typically assume stationarity, rely on fixed thresholds, or adapt only at the population level, causing false alarms during workflow changes and missed events when physiology evolves within and across patients. We propose a new framework for **adaptive outlier detection in streaming clinical data** that jointly models temporal drift, patient-specific context, and uncertainty, enabling detectors to update online without sacrificing statistical control or interpretability. The novelty is a principled combination of sequential representation learning, drift-aware calibration, and decision rules that provide explicit guarantees on alert rate and detection delay under nonstationary conditions, advancing beyond prior heuristic or batch retraining approaches.

**Task 1: Formalize the adaptive detection problem and construct benchmark streams.** We will define a streaming setting with clinically realistic drift modes, censoring, irregular timestamps, and heterogeneous feature scales, then assemble retrospective benchmark streams from de-identified vital signs and laboratory trajectories. **Milestone:** a public evaluation protocol with at least three drift scenarios, two clinical stream types, and baseline comparisons completed by Month 6.

**Task 2: Develop drift-aware online detection algorithms.** We will design methods that update latent state estimates and alert thresholds online using uncertainty-aware calibration and patient-conditioned adaptation. The algorithms will be compared against static thresholds, sliding-window detectors, and standard online anomaly models. **Milestone:** demonstration of at least 20% reduction in false alarms at matched sensitivity, or at least 15% improvement in mean time-to-detection under drift, by Month 15.

**Task 3: Establish rigorous evaluation and interpretability analyses.** We will quantify robustness, calibration error, subgroup stability, computational cost, and explanation fidelity under simulated and retrospective deployment conditions. **Milestone:** evidence that the proposed method sustains pre-specified alert-rate control, processes each update within 100 ms, and yields clinician-readable feature attributions for at least 90% of flagged events by Month 24.

This aim directly addresses NSF **intellectual merit**. **Scientific rigor** is ensured through formal problem definitions, ablation studies, statistical testing, and reproducible benchmarks. **Qualifications of the investigators** are strong because the team combines expertise in machine learning, streaming algorithms, and clinical data science. **Feasibility** is high given access to appropriate datasets, established computational infrastructure, and a milestone-driven plan that de-risks theory, algorithm design, and evaluation in sequence.
