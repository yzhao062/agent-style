### Specific Aim 1: Develop adaptive outlier detection for nonstationary streaming clinical data

This aim asks: **How can outlier detection remain accurate, timely, and interpretable when clinical data streams are heterogeneous, nonstationary, and only partially labeled?** Current methods typically assume fixed thresholds, stationary distributions, or offline retraining, which limits performance when patient physiology, device behavior, and care workflows shift over time. We will develop an adaptive detection framework that jointly models temporal context, uncertainty, and distribution drift so that anomalous observations can be identified before they propagate into downstream decision support.

The central novelty is an online method that unifies three capabilities rarely integrated in prior approaches: patient- and device-aware adaptation, explicit drift detection tied to detector recalibration, and calibrated confidence scores for each alert. Prior streaming approaches often optimize a single objective, such as sensitivity under stationary assumptions or drift handling without clinical interpretability. In contrast, our approach will treat outlier detection as a dynamic inference problem in which baseline behavior evolves across timescales and anomaly scores must remain stable under benign variation while responding rapidly to clinically meaningful deviations.

We will execute three tasks. **Task 1:** Build a benchmark corpus of de-identified streaming vital sign, laboratory, and device telemetry data with injected and expert-validated anomaly patterns. **Milestone:** a reproducible benchmark with at least three data modalities, documented drift scenarios, and baseline performance profiles for at least five comparator methods. **Task 2:** Design adaptive algorithms that combine online representation learning, change-point detection, and uncertainty calibration. **Milestone:** relative improvement of at least 15% in area under the precision-recall curve and at least 20% reduction in alert delay versus strong baselines under simulated and retrospective drift. **Task 3:** Evaluate robustness, computational efficiency, and interpretability in realistic deployment conditions. **Milestone:** sustained throughput compatible with real-time streaming, bounded memory growth, and clinician-reviewable explanations that preserve calibrated detection performance across sites or units.

This aim strongly aligns with NSF **intellectual merit** criteria. **Scientific rigor** is ensured through formal hypotheses, controlled ablations, benchmark release, and statistically principled evaluation under multiple drift regimes. **Qualifications of the investigators** are reflected in the team’s complementary expertise in machine learning, streaming systems, and clinical data analytics, enabling both methodological innovation and domain-grounded validation. **Feasibility** is supported by access to appropriate retrospective datasets, mature baseline methods, and a staged plan with quantitative milestones that de-risk algorithm design and evaluation within the project period.
