## Methods

We model each input graph \(G=(V,E,X)\) with a graph encoder that maps variable-size structures to a fixed-dimensional representation suitable for graph-level anomaly detection. The encoder is a 3-layer Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) with hidden width 128 at every layer. Each message-passing block applies an MLP with ReLU activation and batch normalization, followed by residual addition when dimensions match. To obtain a graph embedding, we concatenate mean and sum pooling over node states from the final layer and project the result to a 128-dimensional latent vector with a linear head. Dropout with rate 0.2 is applied after each block to improve robustness to structural noise. During pretraining, the encoder parameters are optimized with Adam using a learning rate of \(1\times10^{-3}\) and weight decay of \(1\times10^{-5}\).

The encoder is pretrained without labels using a contrastive self-supervised objective that brings semantically consistent views of the same graph together while separating different graphs. For each graph, we generate two positive views by independently sampling lightweight augmentations that preserve coarse semantics: edge dropout with probability 0.2, node-feature masking with probability 0.1, and subgraph sampling that retains 90% of nodes. The two augmented views of the same source graph form a positive pair. Negatives are constructed from embeddings of other graphs in the same mini-batch and from a memory queue of 4096 previously encoded graphs, which increases the diversity of nonmatching examples. We optimize an InfoNCE loss on cosine similarities, with temperature \(\tau=0.2\) and batch size 128. To avoid trivial positives created by near-identical augmentations, we require that at least one structural or attribute perturbation be applied to every view.

At inference, the pretrained encoder is frozen and each graph is embedded once without stochastic augmentation. An anomaly score is then computed in latent space by fitting a Gaussian reference model to the embeddings of the unlabeled training graphs and measuring the Mahalanobis distance of a test embedding \(z\) from the training distribution: \(s(G)=(z-\mu)^\top \Sigma^{-1}(z-\mu)\). We estimate \(\mu\) and \(\Sigma\) from all training embeddings and add a diagonal shrinkage term \(\lambda=10^{-3}\) to \(\Sigma\) for numerical stability. Graphs with larger scores are considered more anomalous because their representations deviate more strongly from the compact manifold learned during contrastive pretraining.
