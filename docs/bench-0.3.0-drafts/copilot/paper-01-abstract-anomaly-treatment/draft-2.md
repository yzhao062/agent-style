Unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection aims to identify rare and structurally atypical graphs when only unlabeled collections of normal-dominant graphs are available, a setting that arises in molecular screening, transaction network analysis, and program behavior monitoring. Existing methods often rely on reconstruction objectives or shallow graph statistics, which can underfit high-order structure and produce representations that are poorly aligned with anomaly scoring. We propose a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework for graph-level anomaly detection that first learns anomaly-sensitive graph embeddings from large unlabeled graph corpora, then scores test graphs by density deviation in the pretrained representation space. Our method combines multi-view graph augmentations that preserve semantic content while perturbing local topology, a hard-negative sampling strategy that emphasizes structurally similar but semantically distinct graphs, and a prototype-regularized contrastive objective that improves cluster compactness for normal graphs.

The resulting model requires no anomaly labels during training and transfers across datasets with minimal adaptation. On TU-MUTAG, our approach improves ROC-AUC from 84.7% to 89.9% over the strongest reconstruction-based baseline, a gain of 5.2 points. On NCI1, it raises average precision from 78.3% to 82.6%, outperforming prior graph autoencoding and one-class graph kernel methods by 4.3 points. On a synthetic-to-real benchmark built from PROTEINS with injected anomalous motifs, it improves recall at 95% precision by 8.1% while reducing false positives by 11.4%. Ablation studies show that contrastive pretraining, hard negatives, and prototype regularization each contribute materially, indicating that representation quality is the key bottleneck in unsupervised graph anomaly detection.
