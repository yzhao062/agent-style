Detecting anomalous graphs without labels is important for applications such as molecular screening, fraud discovery, and program analysis, yet existing unsupervised graph anomaly detectors often optimize reconstruction or neighborhood agreement objectives that do not isolate the rare graph-level factors that make an example abnormal. We study unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection through contrastive self-supervised pretraining, and ask whether representations learned from invariances over normal graph structure can improve separation between typical and atypical graphs at test time. Our method, Contrastive Graph Anomaly Pretraining (CGAP), first pretrains a graph encoder with multi-view contrastive objectives defined over structure-preserving augmentations and hard negatives drawn from motif-disrupting corruptions. It then scores anomalies by combining density estimation in the pretrained embedding space with disagreement between invariant and corruption-sensitive projections, so anomalous graphs are assigned high scores when they are both low-density and unstable under semantics-preserving views. Across eight benchmarks, CGAP outperforms the strongest prior unsupervised baselines by 4.1 AUROC points on average. On AIDS, DHFR, and COX2, it improves AUROC from 71.8, 74.6, and 76.1 to 77.2, 79.0, and 80.3, respectively. On social benchmarks with injected anomalies, it raises AUPRC by 5.4 points on IMDB-BINARY and 4.7 points on REDDIT-BINARY, while cross-dataset pretraining on ZINC yields a further 2.1-point AUROC gain over training from scratch. These results suggest that contrastive pretraining is a simple and effective inductive bias for unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection.
