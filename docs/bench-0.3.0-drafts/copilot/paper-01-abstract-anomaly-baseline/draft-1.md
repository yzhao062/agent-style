Detecting anomalous graphs without labels is a fundamental challenge in domains such as molecular screening, fraud detection, and cybersecurity, where rare but consequential deviations must be identified from graph structure and attributes alone. Existing graph-level anomaly detectors often rely on shallow reconstruction objectives or task-specific assumptions, limiting robustness under distributional heterogeneity and scarce supervision. We propose a fully unsupervised framework that first learns graph representations through contrastive self-supervised pretraining and then scores anomalies in the learned embedding space. Our method constructs paired graph views using structure-preserving augmentations at both node-attribute and topology levels, and optimizes an instance discrimination objective that separates semantically distinct graphs while aligning invariant views of the same graph. After pretraining, anomalous graphs are detected using density-aware scoring over the representation manifold, enabling the model to capture subtle global irregularities without requiring anomalous examples during training.

Across five benchmarks spanning molecules, social networks, and transaction graphs, the proposed approach consistently improves over prior unsupervised baselines. On Tox21, it raises graph-level AUROC from 74.8% to 79.6% (+4.8 points), indicating stronger sensitivity to rare toxic substructures. On MUTAG-anomaly, it improves AUPRC from 61.3% to 67.1% (+5.8 points), suggesting better separation under class imbalance. On the Ethereum-Phishing graph set, it increases Recall@100 from 72.4% to 80.2% (+7.8 points), demonstrating practical gains for high-risk retrieval. We further show that pretraining on mixed unlabeled graphs transfers effectively to unseen anomaly types and remains stable under limited node-feature corruption, supporting contrastive pretraining as a principled foundation for unsupervised graph anomaly detection.
