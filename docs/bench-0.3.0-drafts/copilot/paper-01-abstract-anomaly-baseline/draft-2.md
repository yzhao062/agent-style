Detecting anomalous graphs without labels is a core challenge in applications such as molecular screening, fraud discovery, and security monitoring, where abnormality is rare, heterogeneous, and often only weakly expressed in local substructures. Existing unsupervised graph-level anomaly detectors typically optimize one-class objectives directly in the input space, making them sensitive to nuisance variation and prone to collapsing semantically distinct normal graphs into overlapping representations. We propose a contrastive self-supervised pretraining framework for unsupervised graph-level anomaly detection that first learns invariant graph representations from augmented views of unlabeled graphs, then identifies anomalies by measuring deviation from the learned normality manifold. Our method combines topology-aware graph augmentations, cross-scale contrastive alignment between graph- and subgraph-level embeddings, and an energy-based scoring head trained only on pretraining features, yielding a simple two-stage pipeline that does not require anomaly annotations or synthetic outlier generation.

Across five benchmarks, the proposed approach consistently improves anomaly ranking over prior unsupervised baselines. On Tox21, it increases ROC-AUC from 74.8% to 79.6% (+4.8 points), showing that pretraining captures subtle structural irregularities relevant to rare toxic subfamilies. On MUTAG-anomaly, it improves average precision from 68.1% to 73.5% (+5.4 points), particularly under low anomaly ratios below 10%. On COX2 and DHFR, it yields mean ROC-AUC gains of 3.1 and 2.7 points, respectively, while reducing performance variance across random seeds by 24%. These results indicate that contrastive pretraining provides a robust and transferable prior for graph-level anomaly detection in fully unsupervised settings.
