## Experiments

We evaluate on five widely used graph-anomaly-detection benchmarks that span both citation and review networks. **Cora** is a sparse citation graph of machine-learning papers in which anomalies are defined by mismatched content and local citation structure. **Citeseer** is a noisier citation network with weaker homophily, which makes contextual anomalies harder to separate from normal boundary nodes. **Pubmed** is a larger biomedical citation graph whose longer-range dependencies test whether a method can exploit structure beyond immediate neighbors. **YelpChi** is a review graph built from Yelp metadata, where anomalous nodes correspond to suspicious users or businesses connected through shared behavior patterns. **Amazon** is a co-review network derived from product reviews, with fraudulent reviewer groups creating dense but semantically inconsistent local subgraphs.

We follow the standard semi-supervised setting used in recent prior work. For each dataset, we create a 60/20/20 train, validation, and test split with stratification over node labels, but only normal nodes from the training partition are exposed to the model; anomalous nodes appear only in validation and test. Hyperparameters are selected on the validation split, and final numbers are reported on the held-out test split. We use **AUROC** as the primary metric because it is threshold-free and comparable across datasets, and we report **AUPRC** as a secondary metric because the anomaly rate is low on YelpChi and Amazon. To reduce split variance, we repeat every experiment with **10 random seeds** and report mean and standard deviation; the same seed controls data split, parameter initialization, and minibatch order for all compared methods.

Our method achieves the best average performance across all five datasets, reaching **91.8 AUROC** and **88.6 AUPRC**. On the citation graphs, it scores **93.7** on Cora, **90.8** on Citeseer, and **85.9** on Pubmed, compared with **DOMINANT** at **90.4/87.1/82.6** and **AnomalyDAE** at **91.2/88.0/83.4**. The gains are larger on the review graphs: on YelpChi we obtain **95.1 AUROC**, surpassing **CoLA** at **91.3** and **DOMINANT** at **89.8**; on Amazon we reach **93.4**, versus **89.7** for CoLA and **88.6** for AnomalyDAE. The strongest ablation removes the cross-view consistency term between structural and attribute encoders, which lowers average AUROC from **91.8** to **88.0** and causes the largest single-dataset drop on YelpChi, from **95.1** to **90.0**, showing that aligning the two views is the main source of robustness.
