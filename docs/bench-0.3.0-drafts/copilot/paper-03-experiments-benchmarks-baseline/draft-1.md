We evaluate the proposed method on five widely used graph-anomaly-detection benchmarks that vary in size, homophily, and anomaly type. **Cora** is a citation network of machine-learning papers in which contextual anomalies are formed by perturbing node attributes while preserving sparse citation structure. **Citeseer** is a noisier citation graph with higher feature sparsity, making subtle anomalous nodes harder to separate from rare but legitimate topics. **Pubmed** is a larger biomedical citation network whose denser class neighborhoods test whether anomaly scores remain calibrated at scale. **Amazon** is a co-purchase graph where anomalous products arise from inconsistent metadata and irregular local connectivity patterns. **YelpChi** is a heterogeneous review graph adapted for node-level detection, with fraudulent behavior reflected by both suspicious features and structurally atypical interactions.

For all datasets, we follow the common semi-supervised evaluation protocol in which a small clean subset is used for model selection and the full labeled benchmark is reserved for testing. We randomly split nodes into 10% validation and 90% test after removing benchmark-provided anomaly labels from training, so the encoder is fit in an unsupervised manner on the observed graph and attributes. Hyperparameters are selected by validation AUC, and final performance is reported on the held-out test partition using **ROC-AUC** as the primary metric and **Average Precision (AP)** as a complementary ranking measure because of class imbalance. To reduce variance, we repeat each experiment over **10 random seeds** controlling weight initialization, negative sampling, and split generation, and report mean ± standard deviation.

Across all five datasets, our model achieves the best average performance, reaching **89.7 AUC / 82.4 AP** overall. On Cora, it improves over **DOMINANT** from **84.3** to **88.9 AUC** and over **AnomalyDAE** from **86.1** to **89.4**; on Citeseer, the margin is **87.2** versus **82.8** and **84.6**, respectively. The gains remain consistent on larger graphs, including **91.3 AUC** on Pubmed, **88.1** on Amazon, and **93.0** on YelpChi, where the strongest baseline, **ComGA**, reaches **90.8**. Ablation results show that the largest drop comes from removing the **cross-view contrastive consistency term**, which lowers average AUC from **89.7** to **85.6**, substantially more than removing the adaptive neighborhood reweighting module (**87.4**) or the residual score fusion head (**88.2**). These results indicate that aligning structural and attribute views is the main source of robustness, while the remaining components provide smaller but still consistent gains.
